Tuesday • Lesson 1
The Art of Asking: Prompt Engineering Through Guess Who
March 3, 2026
Do Now
Students consider how the way you ask a question changes what you get back. They compare a vague prompt ("tell me about dogs") to a specific one ("what are three differences between golden retrievers and poodles in terms of temperament?") and answer a multiple-choice question about which prompt would produce the most useful AI response.
Lesson Overview
0-5 min
Warm Up & Key Concepts: Students explore the four keys of prompt engineering (Specificity, Constraints, Context, Iteration) through examples and a multiple-choice question about improving a vague prompt
5-25 min
Guess Who? Game: Students play at least 2 rounds of Guess Who against classmates or AI, discovering that the game is prompt engineering in disguise — precise yes/no questions eliminate uncertainty fast, just like precise prompts get better AI outputs
25-35 min
Reflection — From Game to AI: Students analyze their best and worst questions from the game, connecting game strategy to prompt engineering principles
35-40 min
Exit Ticket: Students write the best AI prompt they can, demonstrating everything they learned about specificity, constraints, context, and iteration
Exit Ticket
Students craft a real-world AI prompt for something they'd actually use (homework help, creative project, learning something new), applying all four prompt engineering keys.
Wednesday • Lesson 2
Gaming: Make Your Case
March 4, 2026
Do Now
Students write a quick 1-2 sentence take on whether gaming is beneficial for young people, then consider the Question of the Day: "What makes a video argument convincing — and how can editing techniques strengthen your message?"
Lesson Overview
0-5 min
Warm Up & Quick Take: Students share their initial opinion on gaming and review learning objectives (thesis development, advanced WeVideo features, video structure, evidence vs. opinion)
5-15 min
Build Your Argument: Students sort 10 claims as FOR or AGAINST gaming, choose their position, and write a thesis statement with a clear reason
15-25 min
Plan Your Video: Students learn the 6-part persuasive video structure (Hook → Thesis → Evidence 1 → Evidence 2 → Counterargument → Conclusion), sketch a quick storyboard, and use the Script Helper AI chatbot to draft talking points
25-30 min
Advanced WeVideo Techniques: Students learn B-roll, text overlays, transitions, and voiceover recording to make their persuasive video more professional
30-40 min
Production Time: Students open WeVideo and begin building their 60-90 second persuasive video, applying the structure and advanced techniques they just learned
Exit Ticket
Students compare their initial warm-up opinion about gaming to the argument in their video, reflecting on how the research and evidence-gathering process changed or strengthened their thinking.
Open WeVideo
Thursday • Lesson 3
Gaming Project: Production Day
March 5, 2026
Do Now
Students review their storyboard from yesterday and rate their video plan readiness on a 1-10 scale. They consider the Question of the Day: "What's the difference between a video that states an opinion and one that persuades?"
Lesson Overview
0-5 min
Check-In & Schedule: Students identify where they are in the production process (starting, building, or refining) and review the project timeline — today is build day, tomorrow is peer review + refinement
5-35 min
Production Time: Students build their persuasive videos in WeVideo (60-90 seconds, Hook → Thesis → Evidence → Counterargument → Conclusion) with time checks and a Production Assistant AI chatbot for support
35-40 min
End-of-Class Check-In: Students describe where their video stands and identify what still needs to happen tomorrow
Exit Ticket
Students describe the hardest part of production today — whether it was finding B-roll, recording voiceover, timing, or structuring the counterargument — and note what help they need for tomorrow.
