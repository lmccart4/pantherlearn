Monday • Lesson 10
Battleship: AI Literacy Review
March 9, 2026
Do Now
Students review the mission briefing for Operation Red Tide — a battleship game where AI literacy knowledge serves as ammunition. They review the key concepts being tested: how generative AI makes predictions, the role of training data, embeddings and attention, and identifying bias in AI systems.
Lesson Overview
0-2 min
Mission Briefing: Students read the rules and objectives — answer AI literacy questions correctly to fire on enemy ships, wrong answers mean a missed round
2-5 min
Review Objectives: Students review the four key areas: how generative AI predicts outputs, the role of training data, how embeddings and attention work, and identifying bias in AI systems
5-35 min
Operation Red Tide: Students play the interactive Battleship game, answering AI literacy questions to earn the right to fire on enemy ships and sink the opposing fleet
35-40 min
Mission Debrief: Students reflect on which AI literacy concepts gave them the most trouble and explain one question they answered incorrectly using proper terminology
Exit Ticket
Students pick one question they got wrong during the game and explain the correct answer and why it’s correct, using vocabulary from earlier lessons. They also preview the next topic: exploring how the words you choose when prompting AI directly shape the output it generates.
Tuesday • Lesson 11
Who’s Making the Choices?
March 10, 2026
Do Now
Students analyze a scenario where two students asked a chatbot about school safety using differently framed prompts and received completely different responses. They identify what changed and connect it to how the attention mechanism processes language differently based on word choice.
Lesson Overview
0-10 min
Warm Up & Discussion: Students examine two differently-framed prompts about school safety, analyze how framing changes AI output, and discuss the “AI Hype Person” vs. “AI Skeptic” perspectives on what this means
10-35 min
Prompt Comparisons Activity: Students experiment with base vs. framed prompts using two AI chatbots — first testing pre-designed prompt pairs, then creating their own variations to see how language shapes AI responses
35-40 min
Check for Understanding: Students answer multiple-choice questions connecting prompt framing to attention mechanisms, then consider the ethical implications of who controls how AI questions are framed
Exit Ticket
Students revisit the AI Hype Person and AI Skeptic perspectives with their new understanding, then select the statement that best captures their takeaway about how prompt language shapes AI output. They also interact with a Prompt Framing Advisor chatbot to solidify their understanding.
Wednesday • Lesson 12
Seeing Attention in Action
March 11, 2026
Do Now
Students read two sentences containing the word “bank” in different contexts (depositing a check vs. a river bank) and identify which specific surrounding words signal each meaning — demonstrating that they naturally do what the attention mechanism does.
Lesson Overview
0-10 min
Warm Up & Connection: Students practice disambiguating words from context, then connect this to how embeddings give words numerical representations and attention dynamically adjusts those representations based on surrounding words
10-35 min
Attention Visualizer: Students work through 9 sentences across 3 stages in the interactive Attention Visualizer — observing how the same word gets different attention patterns depending on context, including ambiguous cases like “bat,” “bank,” and “crane”
35-40 min
Socratic Wrap Up: Students reflect on how the same word produced different attention patterns, discuss the implications for genuinely ambiguous sentences, and review key vocabulary including attention heads, context window, and self-attention
Exit Ticket
Students consider the sentence “She saw the man with the telescope” — a genuinely ambiguous sentence — and explain why even the attention mechanism can’t fully resolve certain ambiguities, connecting this to real-world limitations of language models.
Thursday • Lesson 13
The Hallucination Lab
March 12, 2026
Do Now
Students revisit the attention mechanism concepts from Wednesday, reading two ambiguous sentences and identifying which surrounding words signal meaning — reinforcing how context shapes interpretation before exploring what happens when AI gets context wrong.
Lesson Overview
0-10 min
Warm Up & Review: Students recall how embeddings and attention work together, setting the foundation for understanding why AI sometimes generates confident but incorrect outputs (hallucinations)
10-35 min
Hallucination Lab: Students use the interactive Attention Visualizer to explore edge cases where the attention mechanism struggles — examining how AI processes ambiguous, misleading, or context-poor inputs that can lead to hallucinated outputs
35-40 min
Socratic Wrap Up: Students discuss real-world implications of AI hallucinations, review when and why hallucinations occur, and connect these limitations to the importance of human verification of AI outputs
Exit Ticket
Students reflect on genuinely ambiguous language that even humans struggle with, explaining why AI hallucinations are an inherent feature of probabilistic language models rather than simple “mistakes” that can be easily fixed.
