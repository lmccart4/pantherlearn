Monday • Lesson 1
Data Labeling Lab
March 2, 2026
Do Now
Students are introduced to the Data Labeling Lab — an interactive activity where they take on the role of a data labeler, the humans who prepare training data that AI systems learn from. Students begin labeling data to experience firsthand the tedious, subjective, and sometimes ethically complex work behind AI training.
Lesson Overview
0-5 min
Introduction: Students learn what data labeling is and why it matters — every AI model depends on human-labeled training data
5-30 min
Data Labeling Lab: Students work through the interactive labeling activity, making judgment calls about how to categorize ambiguous data — experiencing the subjective nature of the work that shapes AI behavior
30-40 min
Reflection: Students reflect on how the activity made them feel and what it reveals about the often invisible, low-paid labor force that trains AI systems around the world
Exit Ticket
Students write about their experience doing the labeling activity — how it felt, and what it makes them think about the people (often in developing countries) who do this work at scale for pennies per task.
Tuesday • Lesson 2
The Art of Asking: Prompt Engineering Through Guess Who
March 3, 2026
Do Now
Students consider how the way you ask a question changes what you get back. They compare a vague prompt ("tell me about dogs") to a specific one and answer a multiple-choice question about which prompt would produce the most useful AI response.
Lesson Overview
0-5 min
Warm Up & Key Concepts: Students explore the four keys of prompt engineering (Specificity, Constraints, Context, Iteration) through examples and practice questions
5-25 min
Guess Who? Game: Students play at least 2 rounds of Guess Who against classmates or AI, discovering that precise yes/no questions eliminate uncertainty fast — just like precise prompts produce better AI outputs
25-35 min
Reflection — From Game to AI: Students analyze their best and worst questions from the game and connect game strategy to prompt engineering principles
35-40 min
Exit Ticket: Students write the best AI prompt they can for something they'd actually use, demonstrating specificity, constraints, context, and iteration
Exit Ticket
Students craft a real-world AI prompt for something they'd actually use (homework help, creative project, learning something new), applying all four prompt engineering keys they practiced.

Thursday • Lesson 3
How AI Learns: Intro to Neural Networks
March 5, 2026
Do Now
Students consider how their own brain learns something new — like recognizing a friend's face in a crowd. They review the learning objectives and think about the connection: your brain has about 86 billion neurons, and artificial neural networks are inspired by this same architecture.
Lesson Overview
0-10 min
Warm Up & Brain Connection: Students reflect on how their brain learns, review objectives (explain neural network structure, trace data flow, describe training, connect to real AI), and learn the key insight: neural networks are systems of connected nodes organized in layers
10-35 min
Neural Network Lab (4 experiments): Students work through an interactive lab where they (1) become a neuron, (2) build a network, (3) train it to classify creatures, and (4) observe backpropagation adjusting weights — learning that "knowledge" lives in the connection weights between nodes
35-40 min
Wrap Up & Connections: Students explain where knowledge lives in a neural network (in the weights) and connect yesterday's embeddings lesson to today's neural networks — embeddings turn meaning into numbers, neural networks process those numbers through layers
Exit Ticket
Students connect the dots between embeddings (turning meaning into numbers) and neural networks (processing those numbers through weighted layers) to explain how these two concepts work together in real AI systems.
