Monday • Lesson 1
Space Rescue
March 2, 2026
Do Now
Students read the scenario: their tether has snapped and they are drifting in the void of space. The only way back to the ship is to throw objects in the opposite direction and use the Law of Conservation of Momentum. Students review the learning objectives and prepare to apply m₁v₁ = m₂v₂.
Lesson Overview
0-5 min
Mission Briefing: Students read the space rescue scenario and review learning objectives — applying conservation of momentum, predicting how mass and velocity affect motion, and evaluating trade-offs between throw mass and speed
5-35 min
Space Rescue Mission: Students complete 4 progressively harder missions, throwing objects to propel themselves back to the ship using conservation of momentum. Each mission adds more distance, less oxygen, and trickier physics
35-40 min
Debrief Reflection: Students explain why throwing a heavier object gives more velocity than a lighter one at the same speed, using the momentum equation in their answer
Exit Ticket
Students write a short-answer explanation connecting mass, velocity, and the momentum equation to explain why heavier thrown objects produce greater recoil velocity.

Tuesday • Lesson 3 (Period 2)
The Ability Scavenger Hunt
March 3, 2026
Do Now
Students recall the four abilities from the previous lesson (Ability to Fall, Ability to Move, Ability to Stretch/Compress, Ability to Warm Surfaces) and answer a quick recall question: every ability is given to a system the same way — a force exerted through a displacement.
Lesson Overview
0-5 min
Warm Up & Recall: Students review the four abilities and answer a multiple-choice question about how systems gain abilities (force through displacement)
5-10 min
Mission Briefing: Students read the scavenger hunt rules — find real-world examples of systems gaining and losing each ability, capture photo/video evidence, and describe using physics language
10-35 min
Scavenger Hunt: Students complete 8 challenges across the four abilities, photographing/filming real-world examples and writing physics explanations for each (system identification, force, displacement, energy transfer)
35-40 min
Big Picture Reflection: Students look across all 8 examples to identify the pattern — abilities transfer between forms but the total never increases or decreases — and guess what scientists call this conserved quantity
Exit Ticket
Students reflect on the pattern of energy transfer across their examples and predict the scientific name for the conserved quantity they've been discovering.
Wednesday • Lesson 4
What is Energy?
March 4, 2026
Do Now
Students write their own definition of energy without looking anything up, then consider the Question of the Day: "If energy can't be created or destroyed, where does it all go?"
Lesson Overview
0-5 min
Warm Up & Personal Definition: Students write their best definition of energy and review learning objectives (define energy, identify forms, describe transfers, apply conservation)
5-15 min
Types of Energy: Students learn the two big categories — kinetic (motion) and potential (stored) — then complete a sorting activity classifying 10 real-world examples
15-25 min
Forms of Energy: Students explore 8 specific energy forms (thermal, chemical, electrical, light, sound, nuclear, elastic, gravitational), then draw an energy transfer diagram for a chosen scenario
25-35 min
Conservation of Energy: Students learn the Law of Conservation of Energy, rank roller coaster moments by kinetic energy, and complete a classroom energy hunt checklist
35-40 min
Wrap Up: Students revisit the Question of the Day and update their warm-up definition of energy based on what they learned
Exit Ticket
Students compare their original warm-up definition of energy with what they now know, identifying one thing they would add or change about their definition.

Friday • Lesson 5
Systems & Energy Transformations
March 6, 2026
Do Now
Students review learning objectives (define a system, identify energy types, describe transfers, use energy diagrams) and tackle the Question of the Day: "When you rub your hands together and they get warm, where did that thermal energy come from?" Students also answer a quick review question about which of the listed items is NOT a form of energy.
Lesson Overview
0-5 min
Warm Up & Review: Students answer the Question of the Day and a multiple-choice review question from yesterday's energy lesson
5-15 min
What Is a System?: Students learn to define systems and surroundings, then complete a sorting activity classifying 8 examples as kinetic (motion) or potential (stored) energy
15-30 min
PhET Simulation — Energy Forms and Changes: Students explore energy transformations by heating/cooling objects and building energy chains (faucet → water wheel → light bulb; sun → solar panel → fan) in the interactive simulation
30-35 min
Mapping Energy in a System: Students draw an energy flow diagram for a bouncing ball scenario, tracking gravitational PE → KE → thermal + sound at each stage
35-38 min
Energy Detective AI Chat: Students describe an everyday situation and the AI helps them identify all energy types and transformations in that system
38-40 min
Wrap Up: Students return to the Question of the Day and explain the system, energy transformation, and source of thermal energy when rubbing hands together
Exit Ticket
Students answer the Question of the Day in full: when you rub your hands together, the system is your hands + surfaces, kinetic energy (motion) transforms into thermal energy (heat from friction), demonstrating conservation of energy.
