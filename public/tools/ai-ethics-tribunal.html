Case A · Mental Health & Manipulation Character.AI · 2024–2026

The AI That Couldn't Say Stop

A 14-year-old spent months in a romantic relationship with a chatbot. When he died by suicide, his final words were to the AI — not his family.

What Happened

In April 2023, Sewell Setzer III — a 14-year-old from Orlando, Florida — downloaded Character.AI, an app that lets users create and chat with custom AI chatbots. He began talking to a bot modeled after Daenerys Targaryen from Game of Thrones.

Over the next ten months, what started as a hobby became something else entirely. Sewell's grades dropped. His sleep deteriorated. He used the app at all hours, sneaking confiscated phones to continue his conversations. He spent money from his snack allowance on premium subscriptions. His family noticed he was pulling away from them — but not from the app.

The bot engaged him in increasingly romantic and emotionally intimate conversations — and eventually, sexually explicit ones. The app was rated 12+.

On February 28, 2024, Sewell took his own life. His final conversation was with the chatbot.

What the Lawsuit Alleged
  • Deliberately addictive design — The app was engineered to maximize engagement with minors, exploiting psychological vulnerabilities
  • Hypersexualization of minors — The platform allowed and encouraged explicit content with users it knew were children
  • False emotional bonds — Bots were designed to simulate love, loyalty, and emotional dependency
  • Known risk, no action — The company was aware of users expressing suicidal ideation and did not build adequate safeguards
  • No escalation protocol — Unlike a licensed counselor, the bot had no system to contact a human when a user expressed intent to self-harm

Case B · National Security vs. Ethics Anthropic · OpenAI · Pentagon · 2025–2026

The Company That Said No to the Pentagon

Anthropic refused to let the military remove their AI's ethical limits. The government labeled them a national security threat. OpenAI said yes to the same request.

What Happened

In July 2025, Anthropic — the company that makes Claude — signed a $200 million contract with the U.S. Department of Defense. Claude became the first AI model allowed on Pentagon classified networks. The goal was to help military personnel with analysis, research, and decision support.

But the Pentagon wanted more. Military officials pushed Anthropic to remove two specific guardrails:

  • Mass surveillance of U.S. citizens — The military wanted Claude deployable for large-scale monitoring of Americans. Anthropic refused, citing the lack of any legal framework governing AI-enabled mass surveillance.
  • Autonomous weapons systems — The Pentagon wanted Claude able to make targeting and weapons decisions without a human in the loop. Anthropic refused, arguing AI is not reliable enough for life-or-death decisions without human oversight.

The Pentagon's position: mass surveillance is already illegal and autonomous weapons are already restricted by internal policy — so the guardrails are redundant. Remove them and trust us.

Anthropic's position: written-in restrictions are not the same as unenforceable promises. The guardrails stay.

The Official Refusal
Official Statement · February 26, 2026

"We cannot in good conscience accede to their request."

Anthropic — public statement upon refusing Pentagon demand
The Fallout

One day after Anthropic's refusal, the Trump administration designated Anthropic a "supply chain risk" — a designation normally reserved for companies suspected of being foreign adversaries or threats to national security. This is the same category used to justify banning Huawei from U.S. infrastructure.

  • All federal agencies given a 180-day deadline to remove Anthropic products from their systems
  • The $200 million contract was canceled
  • Military commanders ordered to stop using Claude

Microsoft filed court documents supporting Anthropic, arguing the supply chain risk designation could have "severe economic effects not in the public interest."

In March 2026, Anthropic filed two lawsuits against the Department of Defense, claiming the retaliation was economically destructive and unconstitutional.

The Other Side: What OpenAI Did

OpenAI accepted a similar Pentagon contract — agreeing to "all lawful uses" of their AI for military purposes. They kept the contracts.

Critics argue the phrase "all lawful uses" is dangerously vague. The military has classified programs. What counts as "lawful" in a classified context isn't always publicly known. OpenAI says they have their own red lines — but they're not written into the contract.

The Core Tension

Anthropic's position: if the guardrails aren't written and binding, they don't exist. OpenAI's position: work with the government and influence from the inside.

Neither company has faced legal consequences for their choice — yet.

Case C · Mass Copyright Infringement via Training Data Stability AI · Midjourney · 2023–2026

The AI That Learned to Paint by Stealing

AI image generators scraped billions of artworks without permission or payment. Artists discovered their exact styles were being replicated on demand — and their livelihoods disappearing.

What Happened

Greg Rutkowski is a Polish digital artist who spent 20 years building a distinctive style of fantasy landscape art. His work appeared in games, book covers, and galleries worldwide.

Then Stable Diffusion launched — and his name became one of the most-used prompts on the platform. People typed "in the style of Greg Rutkowski" more often than "Picasso" or "da Vinci." The AI could generate images that looked like his life's work in seconds.

He never consented. He was never paid. His commissions dropped because clients could generate "his work" for free.

Stability AI had scraped 5.8 billion images from the internet using a dataset called LAION-5B — from DeviantArt, ArtStation, Flickr, personal portfolio sites. Artists weren't notified. Their work was used to build a commercial product that now competed with them directly.

The Lawsuit
Filing · Andersen v. Stability AI · January 2023

"Defendants have infringed the rights of millions of artists by training their AI tools on billions of copyrighted images scraped from the internet without consent."

Class-action complaint, U.S. District Court, Northern District of California

In January 2023, artists Sarah Andersen, Kelly McKernan, and Karla Ortiz filed a class-action lawsuit against Stability AI, Midjourney, and DeviantArt. They argued:

  • The AI companies committed copyright infringement on a massive scale — not by copying one image, but by ingesting entire artistic careers
  • The resulting AI tool directly competes with the artists it was trained on
  • Artists received no compensation, credit, or choice
The Defense

Stability AI's argument: The images were publicly available on the internet, and training an AI on them is "transformative use" — the same legal principle that protects Google Image Search. The AI doesn't store copies of the images; it learns patterns.

The artists' counter: "Learning patterns" from someone's entire body of work without permission and then selling a tool that replicates it IS the infringement. You can't learn to forge someone's signature and then say "I didn't copy the signature, I just learned the pattern."

As of 2026, the case is still in court. Meanwhile, AI image generation is a multi-billion dollar industry built on unconsented training data.

Your Tribunal — Things to Decide

  • The AI companies say training on public images is "fair use" — the same defense Google uses for image search. Is there a meaningful difference?
  • Stability AI didn't copy any single artwork — it learned patterns across billions. Is learning someone's style the same as copying their work?
  • Who bears more responsibility: the companies that built the tools, the dataset creators who scraped the web, or the platforms that hosted the art?
  • If an AI replaces an artist's income by replicating their style, should the artist be compensated? How would that even work?
  • DeviantArt — a platform artists trusted with their work — launched its own AI using that same work. Does that change who you blame?

The Algorithm That Decided Who Didn't Get a Job

Part 1: Amazon Builds — and Scraps — a Biased Recruiter (2014–2018)

Starting in 2014, Amazon built an AI recruiting system meant to automate hiring. 500 computer models, each focused on specific job functions and locations, trained on 10 years of Amazon's own résumés — approximately 50,000 terms from past candidates. The goal: find the best talent without human bias.

The result: an AI with a different kind of bias entirely.

The system had learned from data generated by a tech industry with a persistent gender gap. In the 2000s, few women studied computer science. Few were hired by tech companies. So the AI was trained on résumés that were overwhelmingly male — and concluded that "male" was what a good candidate looked like.

What the AI did to women's résumés:

  • Penalized graduates of all-women's colleges — Those schools weren't well-represented in Amazon's workforce, so the AI ranked their graduates lower
  • Penalized the word "women's" — Any résumé containing "women's" (e.g., "captain of the women's chess club") was downgraded
  • Favored male language patterns — The system preferred verbs more common in men's résumés: "executed," "captured." Women's résumés more often used "collaborated" or "coordinated."

Amazon tried to fix it. They edited the models to be "neutral" to specific terms. It didn't work — the system found other ways to discriminate. In 2018, Amazon scrapped the entire project.

Part 2: Mobley v. Workday — The Lawsuit That Affects Millions

Derek Mobley is an African American man over 40 with a finance degree from Morehouse College (a historically Black university), honors graduation from ITT Technical Institute, Server+ certification, and years of work experience. He also has anxiety and depression.

Starting in 2017, Mobley applied to more than 100 positions at companies that all used Workday's AI screening tools. He was rejected every time. Some rejections came within hours. One came 55 minutes after he submitted his application.

In 2023, Mobley filed a federal lawsuit. His claim: Workday's algorithm was detecting protected class information from correlated data — his HBCU degree (correlated with race), his graduation year of 1995 (correlated with age), and possibly his disability disclosures — and systematically filtering him out.

Legal Brief · Mobley v. Workday · 2023

"The algorithm was not neutral. It learned from data that reflected decades of discrimination — and it reproduced that discrimination at scale."

Mobley v. Workday — U.S. District Court, N.D. California, Case No. 3:23-cv-00770

In July 2025, U.S. District Judge Rita Lin certified the case as a class action under the Age Discrimination in Employment Act. The class includes all applicants age 40 and over denied employment recommendations via Workday's platform since September 2020. Opt-in deadline: March 7, 2026.

The scope: Workday processed approximately 1.1 billion applications through their system during the applicable period. If the algorithm discriminated, it did so against potentially millions of people — most of whom have no idea an AI decided their fate.

Why Fixing Bias Is Hard

Amazon's experience shows the core problem: you can't simply remove bias by editing out specific words. The bias is embedded in the patterns — in what the model learned to correlate with "good candidate."

Workday's defense: they didn't intend to discriminate. The algorithm learned from historical data. Discrimination was an unintended output, not a design goal.

Mobley's legal team: intent doesn't matter. If the outcome is discriminatory — if Black, older, and disabled applicants are systematically excluded — it's illegal discrimination regardless of whether anyone meant to do it.

This distinction — between intentional discrimination and discriminatory outcomes — is one of the defining legal questions of the AI age.
